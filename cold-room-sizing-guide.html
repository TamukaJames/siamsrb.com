Cold Room Sizing Guide for Zambian Businesses

Getting the size of your cold room right is one of the most important decisions you'll make when investing in refrigeration. Build it too small and you'll be turning away stock and watching produce spoil; build it too big and you'll pay to cool empty space every month — a painful cost when ZESCO tariffs and load-shedding already squeeze your power budget. This guide walks Zambian businesses through how to size a cold room or walk-in freezer correctly the first time.

Why Correct Sizing Matters

A cold room is a long-term investment, and the wrong size costs you for years. Too small, and you'll overpack the room — which blocks airflow, creates warm spots, shortens shelf life and risks the very spoilage you bought it to prevent. Too big, and your refrigeration plant has to chill cubic metres of empty air, driving up your electricity bill and your running costs unnecessarily.

For Zambian operations — butcheries, supermarkets, lodges, restaurants, farms and pharmacies — getting this balance right protects both your stock and your margins. The goal is a room sized to your real product volume with a sensible allowance for growth, no more and no less.

What to Measure Before You Build

Before anyone talks dimensions, gather the facts about what you actually store. The key figures are:

  • Product volume: The maximum quantity (in kilograms or cubic metres) you need to hold at peak, not on a quiet day.
  • Daily turnover: How much product comes in and goes out each day. High turnover means more door openings and warm air entering, which affects the cooling load.
  • Stacking and shelving: Whether goods are palletised, boxed or hung. A butchery hanging carcasses needs height; a supermarket storing boxed produce needs shelf depth.
  • Packaging: Loose produce, crates and pallets all use space differently and trap or allow air differently.

Write these down honestly. Underestimating peak volume is the most common mistake, and it's far cheaper to plan for it now than to build a second room later.

Chilled vs Frozen Requirements

Not all cold storage is equal, and the temperature you need changes the design significantly.

Chilled rooms (2–8°C)

Used for fresh produce, dairy, drinks, fresh meat and pharmaceuticals. These run at fridge temperatures and need a moderate cooling capacity and standard insulation.

Frozen rooms (−18°C and below)

Used for frozen meat, fish and long-term storage. A walk-in freezer needs thicker insulation, a more powerful refrigeration plant and a heated door frame to stop ice forming and sealing the door shut. Many businesses need both — a chilled section and a separate freezer — which is best planned together from the start. Our overview of how a cold room compares to chest freezers can help you decide whether you've outgrown standalone units.

Airflow, Shelving and Walkways

A cold room is not just a box you fill to the ceiling. Cold air must circulate freely around every item to keep temperatures even, so you have to design in empty space:

  • Leave clearance around the evaporator (the cooling unit) so air can flow.
  • Keep gaps between stacked goods and the walls.
  • Allow proper walkways so staff can load and pick stock safely without propping the door open for ages.

A practical rule is that usable storage is only about 60–70% of the room's floor area once you account for airflow and access. Factor that in, or your "big enough" room will feel cramped on day one.

Quick tip: Size your refrigeration plant for Zambia's hot season, not an average day. A unit that copes in July may struggle through an October heatwave with the door opening all day. Building in extra capacity also means the room recovers temperature faster after a load-shedding outage.

Insulation Panels and Door Type

The insulated panels are the shell that holds the cold in, and their thickness is matched to the temperature. As a general guide, chilled rooms commonly use 75–100mm panels, while freezer rooms use 100–150mm or thicker. Skimping on panel thickness to save money up front means a plant that runs harder, higher bills forever, and condensation problems in our humid rainy season.

The door matters just as much. A well-fitted, properly sealed door with the right gasket — and a heater frame on freezers — prevents the cold leaks and ice build-up that waste energy. For busy operations, strip curtains or a fast-acting door reduce the warm air pouring in every time staff walk through.

Planning for Future Growth

Most businesses grow, and your cold room should not become the thing that holds you back. When sizing, ask where you expect to be in three to five years. A modest allowance for growth now — slightly larger footprint, or a layout that allows an extension — is far cheaper than rebuilding. At the same time, don't over-build wildly "just in case"; every extra cubic metre is energy you pay to cool every single day. The sweet spot is realistic headroom, not a cavern.

A correctly sized cold room protects your stock, controls your electricity costs and gives you room to grow. Measure your real peak volume, decide chilled versus frozen, design in airflow and access, choose the right panels and door, and allow sensible headroom for the future.
