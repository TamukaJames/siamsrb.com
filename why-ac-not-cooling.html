Why Your Air Conditioner Isn't Cooling (and How to Fix It)

There are few worse moments in a Zambian October than switching on the aircon and feeling warm air blow out. Before you panic — or call out a technician — it helps to understand why an air conditioner stops cooling. Some causes you can fix yourself in minutes; others need a professional. This guide walks through the common reasons an AC blows warm air and tells you, for each one, whether it's a do-it-yourself job or a call to the pros.

Common causes

  • Dirty or blocked filters
  • Low or leaking refrigerant
  • Frozen evaporator coils
  • Dirty outdoor condenser
  • Wrong thermostat or remote settings
  • Failing capacitor or compressor
  • Tripped breaker or electrical fault

Dirty or Blocked Filters

This is by far the most common cause, and the easiest to fix. In Zambia's dusty dry season, the mesh filters inside the indoor unit clog quickly. Blocked filters choke airflow, so even though the system is working, very little cool air reaches the room — and the unit may eventually ice over.

DIY or technician?

This one is DIY. Switch the unit off, slide out the filters, rinse or brush off the dust, let them dry fully in the shade and refit. In dusty conditions, clean them every two to four weeks. Our full AC maintenance guide walks through the routine step by step.

Low or Leaking Refrigerant

Refrigerant (the cooling gas) is what actually removes heat from your room. If the system is low — almost always because of a leak, since refrigerant is not "used up" in a sealed system — the air will blow cool-ish at best, then warm. You may also notice ice on the copper pipes or a hissing sound.

DIY or technician?

Technician only. Handling refrigerant is regulated, licensed work. A professional must find and repair the leak, then recharge the system to the correct level — simply "topping up" without fixing the leak wastes gas and damages the compressor.

Frozen Evaporator Coils

If you see ice on the indoor coils or pipes, the evaporator has frozen. This is usually a symptom of restricted airflow (dirty filters) or low refrigerant. A frozen coil cannot absorb heat, so the unit blows warm even while ice builds up inside it.

DIY or technician?

Partly DIY. Turn the unit off and let the ice melt completely — running the fan-only mode helps. Then clean the filters. If it freezes again after that, the cause is deeper (refrigerant or a fault) and you need a technician.

Quick tip: Never chip or scrape ice off the coils — you can puncture them and cause an expensive refrigerant leak. Always let ice melt naturally with the unit switched off.

Dirty Outdoor Condenser

The outdoor unit dumps the heat your aircon removes from the room. Over a Zambian dry season its fins pack with dust, grass and insects, and it loses the ability to shed heat — so the whole system cools poorly, especially on the hottest afternoons. Plants, walls or stored items crowding the unit make it worse.

DIY or technician?

Partly DIY. You can clear leaves and debris from around the unit and keep at least half a metre of clear space on all sides. But deep coil cleaning and fin straightening are best left to a technician with the right tools.

Wrong Thermostat or Remote Settings

Sometimes the fix is embarrassingly simple. The remote may be set to "Fan" or "Dry" instead of "Cool", the temperature may be set too high, or the mode may have switched after a power cut. With ZESCO outages flipping units off and on, settings can reset themselves.

DIY or technician?

Pure DIY. Check the remote: select Cool mode, set the temperature to around 22–24°C, and make sure the fan is on auto. Always rule this out before assuming a fault.

Failing Capacitor or Compressor

The compressor is the heart of the system, and the capacitor gives it the jolt it needs to start. If the unit hums but won't start cooling, trips repeatedly, or the outdoor fan spins while the compressor stays silent, a failing capacitor or compressor is a likely cause. Zambia's power surges after load-shedding are hard on these components.

DIY or technician?

Technician only. Capacitors store a dangerous charge and compressors are high-voltage. This is skilled, potentially hazardous work — call a qualified AC repair service.

Tripped Breaker or Electrical Fault

If the unit is completely dead, check whether the breaker has tripped — a common after-effect of a ZESCO surge. A single reset may restore it. But if it trips again immediately, that is a warning sign of a fault, not something to keep resetting.

DIY or technician?

One reset is DIY; repeated tripping needs a technician. A breaker that keeps tripping is protecting you from a real electrical fault and must be investigated. If your unit has died suddenly in the heat, our emergency call-out team can respond quickly.

When to Call a Professional

Start with the simple checks — settings, filters, a clear outdoor unit and a breaker reset. These solve a large share of "warm air" complaints at no cost. But if the air is still not cold, if you see ice, hear hissing or grinding, or the breaker keeps tripping, stop and call a professional before you damage the unit further. Persistent poor cooling can also mean the system was simply too small for the space from the start — our guide on choosing the right capacity explains how an undersized unit can never keep up with the October heat.
