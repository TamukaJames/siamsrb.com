Choosing the Right Generator for Your Home or Business

With ZESCO load-shedding now a routine part of life across Zambia, a generator has moved from a luxury to a necessity. But buying the wrong unit is an expensive mistake — too small and it stalls under load, too big and you waste money on fuel and capital. This guide walks you through sizing, fuel choice, installation and the safety details that matter most in Lusaka, the Copperbelt and beyond.

Why a Generator Is Essential During Load-Shedding

Zambia's power supply has come under serious strain in recent years, and many homes and businesses now face long daily blackouts. For a household, that means lost food in the freezer, no lights and no water if you rely on an electric pump. For a business, the stakes are far higher: a butchery loses an entire cold room of meat, a lodge sends guests to bed in the dark, a pharmacy risks its vaccine stock, and a supermarket watches its refrigerated aisles climb past safe temperatures. A reliable generator turns these blackouts into a non-event. It is the most direct way to protect both comfort and revenue, and for many businesses it has become the single most important piece of equipment they own. It is one of several backup power options worth weighing up before you commit.

How to Size Your Generator

Sizing is where most buyers go wrong. A generator that is too small will trip, stall or burn out when it is asked to power more than it can handle. The goal is to estimate your total electrical load in watts (or kVA) and choose a unit with comfortable headroom.

Start by listing everything you want to run at the same time and adding up the wattage. A few rough Zambian-household figures: lights (5–15W each), a TV (80–150W), a fridge (150–400W running), a deep freezer (200–500W running), a borehole pump (750–1,500W) and a typical split air conditioner (900–2,000W running).

The critical detail is the difference between running watts and starting watts. Anything with a motor — fridges, freezers, air conditioners, pumps and compressors — draws a large surge of power for the first second or two as the motor spins up, often two to three times its running figure. A freezer that runs at 300W may demand 900W to start. Your generator must be able to handle the running total plus the largest single starting surge. As a practical rule, add up your running watts, then add the highest starting surge on the list, and choose a generator rated comfortably above that number. To convert to kVA (how generators are usually sold here), divide the total watts by roughly 800. If you are unsure, our team will do a proper load calculation rather than leaving you to guess.

Quick tip: Never size a generator to its absolute maximum. Aim to load it to around 70–80% of its rated capacity. This leaves headroom for motor surges and lets the engine run cooler and longer.

Petrol vs Diesel

The two common fuel choices each suit a different job. Petrol generators are cheaper to buy, lighter and quieter, and they make sense for homes and small offices that need a few hours of backup at a time. Their downsides are higher running costs, a shorter engine life and the need to refuel more often.

Diesel generators cost more upfront but are built for heavy, sustained use. They burn fuel more efficiently, last far longer, and handle the long daily run-times that Zambian load-shedding demands. For any serious commercial site — a lodge, supermarket, farm or workshop — diesel is almost always the right answer. The fuel is also widely available across the country, and diesel tanks can be sized to ride out extended outages.

Portable vs Standby / Whole-Building

A portable generator is wheeled out, started by hand and connected by extension leads or a manual changeover. It is affordable and flexible, ideal for a home that needs to keep the fridge, lights and a few sockets alive. The trade-offs are manual starting, limited capacity and the temptation to run dangerous extension-lead setups.

A standby or whole-building generator is permanently installed, wired into your distribution board and sized to carry the whole property. Paired with an automatic transfer switch, it starts on its own within seconds of a power cut and shuts down when the grid returns — no one has to be home. This is the standard for businesses that cannot tolerate downtime and for larger homes. Our generator supply and installation service covers both ends of this spectrum.

Automatic Transfer Switches and Changeover

However good your generator is, it must never feed power back into the ZESCO line — that can electrocute the linesmen working to restore your supply. This is what a changeover switch prevents. A manual changeover is a switch you physically flip to move the building from grid to generator and back. It is inexpensive and reliable but requires someone present.

An automatic transfer switch (ATS) does the same job instantly and on its own: it senses the outage, signals a standby generator to start, switches the load over, and reverses the process when ZESCO returns. For shops, clinics, lodges and any unattended site, an ATS is well worth the investment. Whichever you choose, fitting a proper changeover is a legal and safety requirement, not an optional extra.

Fuel, Noise, Placement and Ventilation

Generators produce exhaust fumes, heat and noise, so where you put one matters as much as which one you buy. Never run a generator indoors or in an enclosed garage — carbon monoxide is odourless and deadly. Site the unit outside, on a level base, under a ventilated canopy that keeps off the rain and dry-season dust but still allows free airflow for cooling. Keep it well clear of windows and doors so fumes do not drift inside, and leave space around it for servicing.

Noise is a real consideration for guesthouses, residential plots and busy neighbourhoods; a soundproofed canopy makes a big difference. Plan your fuel storage too: keep diesel in approved containers away from the engine, and factor refuelling into your routine so you are never caught short during a long outage.

Why Professional Installation Matters

A generator is a high-current electrical machine, and the wiring, earthing and changeover must be done correctly. Poor earthing leaves the frame live and dangerous; an incorrect changeover risks back-feeding the grid; undersized cabling overheats. These are not jobs for a handyman. A professional installation ensures your generator is correctly connected to the distribution board, properly earthed, fitted with the right protection and tested under load. Once it is running, follow a simple generator maintenance routine to keep it reliable. We install and service generators for homes and businesses in Lusaka, the Copperbelt and tourism towns such as Livingstone.

Choose Once, Choose Right

The right generator is the one matched honestly to your real load, fuelled appropriately for how long you need to run it, and installed safely with a proper changeover. Get those three things right and you will ride out every blackout without drama. Get them wrong and you will pay for it in stalled units, wasted fuel or a dangerous installation.
