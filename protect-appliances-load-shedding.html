How to Protect Your Appliances During Load-Shedding in Zambia

Load-shedding has become a fact of daily life across Zambia, and ZESCO's rolling power cuts do more than disrupt your routine — they quietly damage your fridges, air conditioners, televisions and computers. The danger isn't only the outage itself; it's the voltage spike that hits when the power suddenly comes back. With a few sensible habits and the right protective equipment, you can shield your appliances from both.

Why Load-Shedding Damages Appliances

People assume an appliance is safe simply because it's switched off when the power goes. The real harm happens at two moments. First, the grid often goes through low-voltage "brownouts" before a cut, and running motors — like those in fridges and AC units — struggle and overheat on under-voltage. Second, and worse, when ZESCO restores supply the voltage frequently spikes far above the normal 230 volts for a split second.

That surge is brutal on electronics. Circuit boards in modern fridges, inverter air conditioners, decoders and computers are especially vulnerable. Repeated surges shorten the life of compressors and can destroy a control board outright. In short, it's the return of power, not its loss, that kills most appliances during load-shedding.

The "Wait 3–5 Minutes" Rule

This is the single most important free habit you can adopt. When the power comes back, do not let your fridge, freezer or air conditioner restart immediately. A compressor that tries to start against high internal pressure — before the refrigerant has had time to equalise — can stall, overheat and burn out.

The fix is simple: switch fridges, freezers and AC units off at the wall during a power cut, and switch them back on only 3 to 5 minutes after the power has returned and stabilised. Better still, fit a time-delay relay or a fridge guard that does this automatically. For a butchery or restaurant with several cold appliances, automatic delay protection is well worth the small cost.

Quick tip: Keep fridge and freezer doors shut during an outage. A full freezer can hold safe temperatures for up to 24–48 hours if left closed, so resist the urge to keep checking — every opening lets the cold escape and forces the compressor to work harder once power returns.

Surge Protectors and Voltage Stabilizers (AVRs)

Two different devices do two different jobs, and many Zambian homes need both.

Surge protectors

A surge protector absorbs sudden voltage spikes — exactly the kind that hit when power is restored. Use proper surge-protected multi-plugs (not cheap unfiltered adaptors) for TVs, decoders, computers, routers and sound systems. They are inexpensive and replaceable, and they take the hit so your electronics don't.

Automatic Voltage Regulators / stabilizers

An AVR or voltage stabilizer continuously corrects the incoming voltage, smoothing out both the brownouts and the over-voltage that are common on the Zambian grid. Larger or more sensitive appliances — fridges, freezers, inverter AC units, deep freezers in shops — benefit from a stabilizer rated for their load. A correctly sized AVR is one of the best investments you can make to protect expensive cooling equipment. Our team can advise on and supply electrical protection suited to your appliances.

Unplug Sensitive Electronics During Outages

The cheapest, most reliable protection costs nothing: physically unplug it. During load-shedding, unplug televisions, computers, gaming consoles, decoders and chargers from the wall. A surge can't travel down a cable that isn't connected. This matters most during the rainy season, when lightning adds another source of damaging surges on top of the usual ZESCO switching.

For devices you can't easily unplug, our surge protection guide explains how to layer protection so your electronics are covered even when you're not home to pull the plug.

Backup Power Options

If load-shedding is hurting your home or, more seriously, your business, backup power both keeps you running and reduces the number of harsh on-off cycles your appliances endure. The main options are:

  • Inverter + battery systems: Quiet, automatic switchover, ideal for lights, fridges, Wi-Fi, electronics and small offices. They also clean up the power going to your devices.
  • Solar with battery storage: A longer-term answer that beats the cost of fuel and runs all day in Zambia's strong sunshine.
  • Standby generators: Best for heavier loads — supermarkets, lodges, farms and cold rooms that simply cannot lose power.

The right choice depends on what you need to keep running and for how long. Our guide to backup power options in Zambia compares them in detail, and we design and install generators and backup power systems sized to your exact load.

Whole-House Protection at the Distribution Board

Plug-in protectors guard individual appliances, but the strongest defence is a surge protection device (SPD) installed inside your main distribution board by a qualified electrician. This protects everything in the building from a single point and catches large surges before they reach any socket.

Combined with a properly earthed system and the right circuit breakers, a board-level SPD is the gold standard for surge-prone areas. For businesses with valuable stock and equipment, it's not a luxury — it's basic risk management. A registered electrician should always carry out this work; it involves your main supply and must be done safely and to standard.

Stay Protected, Whatever ZESCO Does

You can't control load-shedding, but you can control how much it costs you. Adopt the wait rule, fit surge protectors and a stabilizer, unplug electronics during cuts, consider backup power, and protect the whole building at the distribution board. Layer these defences and your fridges, AC and electronics will outlast many power cuts to come.
