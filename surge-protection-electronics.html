Surge Protection: Save Your Electronics from Power Cuts

In Zambia, the most dangerous moment for your electronics is not when the power goes off — it is when it comes back on. The voltage spike that follows load-shedding, combined with rainy-season lightning, destroys fridges, air conditioners, televisions and computers across the country every year. The fix is straightforward and far cheaper than replacing your appliances: proper surge protection, fitted in layers and backed by good earthing.

What a Power Surge Is — and Why Zambia Sees So Many

A power surge is a brief, sharp jump in voltage above the normal 230 volts our grid is meant to supply. It can last only a fraction of a second, but in that instant the spike pushes far more energy into your wiring and appliances than they are designed to handle. Zambia is particularly prone to surges for two reasons.

The first is load-shedding. When ZESCO restores power after an outage, the grid often comes back with an unstable, higher-than-normal voltage as supply is switched on across a whole area at once. Anything you left plugged in takes the full force of that spike. The second is weather: Zambia's rainy season brings frequent, intense thunderstorms, and a lightning strike on or near a power line sends an enormous surge racing through the cables into homes and businesses. Add the everyday voltage dips and brownouts common on a stressed grid, and equipment here lives a hard electrical life.

The Damage Surges Do to Your Equipment

Surges harm electronics in two ways. A single large spike — from lightning or a bad switch-on — can instantly fry circuit boards, compressors and power supplies. But smaller, repeated surges are just as costly over time: they slowly degrade components until something fails weeks or months later, long after you have forgotten the storm that started it.

Refrigerators and air conditioners are especially vulnerable because their compressors and control boards are expensive and sensitive to voltage. A surge can also wipe out a flat-screen TV's power board, corrupt or destroy a computer (and the data on it), and kill the chargers and modems that keep a home or office running. For a butchery, lodge, pharmacy or supermarket, a surge that takes out a cold room or a fridge fleet doesn't just cost the equipment — it can spoil thousands of kwacha of stock. This is exactly why surge protection belongs in any serious plan for protecting appliances during load-shedding.

Layers of Protection That Actually Work

No single device protects against everything. The reliable approach is to layer protection so each stage catches what the previous one lets through:

  • Plug-in surge protectors: The first, cheapest line of defence for individual TVs, decoders, computers and chargers. Buy quality units with a real joule rating — cheap multi-plugs labelled "surge" often offer little. Replace them after a major surge, as their protection wears out.
  • AVRs / voltage stabilizers: An Automatic Voltage Regulator smooths out both spikes and the low-voltage dips that are common here, feeding fridges, AC units and electronics a steady supply. An AVR is one of the best investments for any household or business on an unstable line.
  • UPS for sensitive electronics: An Uninterruptible Power Supply both filters the power and rides through the gap when the grid drops, so computers, tills, security systems and networking gear shut down safely instead of crashing.
  • Whole-house surge protection at the distribution board: A surge protection device (SPD) fitted in your DB board intercepts large surges — including many lightning-induced ones — before they spread to every socket in the building. It is the foundation the smaller devices sit on top of.

The same equipment that protects against surges also pairs naturally with backup power, so your home or business stays both safe and running through an outage.

Simple habit: During a storm, or when load-shedding hits, unplug what you can — TVs, computers, sensitive electronics. Nothing protects an appliance better than not being connected when the surge arrives. For everything you can't unplug, fit proper protection.

Why Proper Earthing Matters

Surge protection devices work by diverting excess energy safely to earth. If the earthing in your building is poor, missing or corroded — as it is in many older Zambian properties — that energy has nowhere safe to go, and even the best surge protector can't do its job. A solid earth is also the difference between a surge protector quietly saving your equipment and a fault becoming a shock or fire hazard. Having a qualified electrician test and, where needed, upgrade your earthing is the single most important step that makes all your other protection effective.

Lightning Season and Professional Installation

From around November to April, take extra care. Lightning surges are the most destructive of all, and a strike doesn't need to hit your building directly — a strike on the line down the road can still send a damaging spike your way. A whole-house SPD, good earthing and the habit of unplugging during storms together give you the best defence the rainy season demands.

Choosing and sizing surge protection, fitting an SPD into a live distribution board, and verifying earthing are all jobs for a qualified electrician, not a weekend project. Done wrong, they give a false sense of safety while leaving your equipment exposed.
